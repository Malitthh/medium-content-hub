Chapter 01 · Car Control

Master the machine before
you master the track

Car control is the absolute foundation of sim racing. Without it, no knowledge of racing lines or strategy will save you. Every input you make either works with physics or against it — and physics always wins.

The Traction Budget

Think of each tyre as having exactly 100% of grip to spend at any given moment. The golden rule of car control is this: never ask a tyre to do two things at full intensity simultaneously. If you are using 80% of that grip for braking, you have only 20% remaining for turning. Demand 100% of both at the same moment, and the tyre gives up — you slide, understeer, or spin.

The Golden Sequence

Brake fully → release the brake → turn in → unwind the wheel → apply gas. Each action receives the tyre's complete attention. This is not just sim theory — it is the physics of every real road and circuit on the planet.

Weight Transfer

Every input you make shifts the car's weight between its four tyres. Understanding weight transfer lets you predict and prevent slides before they happen, rather than reacting to them after.

01
Braking

Weight shifts forward. Front grip increases, rear grip decreases. Hard enough and the rear becomes light — snap oversteer risk on corner entry.

02
Acceleration

Weight shifts rearward. Rear grip increases but the front lightens — understeer risk. Too aggressive on exit causes wheelspin or a snap.

03
Cornering

Weight shifts to the outside tyres. The inside tyres unload. This is why curb riding works — the outside tyre handles the load anyway.

Throttle, Steering & Braking

Throttle: Use the full pedal travel. Most beginners operate at 50–70% without realising it. On corner exit, roll the throttle in progressively. Slamming it mid-corner transfers weight rearward suddenly — the rear steps out.

Trail throttle and lift-off oversteer: Releasing the throttle suddenly mid-corner shifts weight forward, unloading the rear. The rear can snap out. Manage this by being smooth with both the throttle application and release.

Steering: Smooth is fast. Jerky, aggressive steering upsets car balance and scrubs speed. When the rear slides, countersteer into the slide — steer right if the rear is going right. This must become instinct.

Threshold braking: Apply the brake as hard as possible without locking the wheels. The point just before lock is peak braking performance. Cross it and you lose steering entirely as the locked wheel slides.

Trail Braking

Gradually releasing brake pressure as you turn in — rather than completing all braking before the corner — keeps weight on the front tyres, improving rotation and corner-entry grip. It is one of the highest-impact advanced techniques and applies directly in a real car on a track day.

Chapter 02 · The Racing Line

The fastest path is never
the shortest path

The racing line is the fastest route through a corner. It minimises the change of direction required, allowing higher speed. Get it right and you carry more speed everywhere — not just the one corner.

Out — In — Out

This single principle describes your position on track, but it is the same idea as Entry → Apex → Exit and Brake → Turn → Gas. Three phrases, one concept, viewed from three angles.

OUT
Position

Start wide on the outside of the track before the turn. This sets up the largest possible arc through the corner.

IN
Apex

Cut to the inside — the apex. The geometric point you clip. Late apex is almost always faster than geometric apex.

OUT
Exit

Track out wide again as you unwind the wheel and apply progressive throttle. Let the car naturally use all the road.

Why This Works

By starting wide, cutting in, and exiting wide you make the corner as large a circle as possible. A larger radius circle requires less steering angle — and less steering means you can carry more speed. The tighter you cut a corner, the slower you must go.

Geometric vs. Late Apex

Geometric apex: The mathematical centre of the corner. Adequate for isolated turns with nothing significant after them.

Late apex: You turn in later than feels natural, hit the apex later, and as a result the exit is straighter sooner. This is almost always faster because it lets you apply full throttle earlier — and earlier throttle means higher speed down the next straight. A driver who can get to full throttle three car lengths earlier than you will be faster in every lap, regardless of corner speed.

The Key Insight

Most beginners lose time on the exit, not the entry. Getting to full throttle 0.2 seconds earlier has more impact than any amount of tweaking entry speed. Always sacrifice entry to optimise exit — especially before a long straight.

Corner Combinations

Chicanes: Sacrifice the first part to optimise the second. The exit corner is the one that matters — it connects to the straight.

Double apex: Long sweeping corners with two apex points. Hold wide through the first half to maintain speed and set up the second apex cleanly. Turning in too early runs you wide and kills the second apex completely.

Hairpins: Almost always use a very late apex. The straight following is typically long, so getting the power down early is crucial. Hairpins reward patience more than any other corner type.

Chapter 02 (Continued) · Reading the Track

5 visual cues that replace
memorisation

Most beginners think learning a track means memorising every braking point by number. That is the hard way. The fast way is learning to read what the track is telling you. Every circuit has a visual language — master it and you can navigate an unfamiliar track with confidence from the very first lap.

1
Brake markers — direction indicator, not just distance

The 100m, 50m, and 25m boards are most commonly associated with braking distance, but their placement on the track tells you something equally important: which side of the track you should be on before the corner arrives. Markers on the left mean you should be positioned left — a right-hand turn is coming. Markers on the right, vice versa. Before you even see the corner, the boards are pointing you toward the correct line. Sim Watch for board placement in cockpit cam. Real world Applies identically on track days.

2
Kerbing — corners announce themselves

Kerbs are placed exactly where a car on the correct racing line would be. They are not random — circuit designers position them to reward the right line and discourage the wrong one. If you see kerbing appear suddenly on what looked like a straight, a corner is about to arrive. Begin your preparation: position the car, initiate trail braking, and locate your turn-in reference. Especially useful on high-speed circuits like Spa or Suzuka where corners arrive quickly. Sim Visible in all major titles — cockpit view gives the best read. Real world Kerbs mark the ideal line precisely.

3
Braking requirement — hard brake vs. lift vs. feather

Not every corner requires the same braking input. Distance boards present means hard braking — full pressure, trail to apex. No boards with slight compression means a lift corner — simply ease off throttle, perhaps a feather of brake. No boards and flat means full throttle or near-flat — a commitment corner. Learning to distinguish these reduces lap time enormously. Committing to a flat corner instead of panic-braking saves large amounts of time across a lap. Sim Force feedback will confirm if you over-braked — you will understeer into the apex.

4
Speed via gear selection — use gears, not the speedometer

Looking at your speedometer mid-corner takes your eyes off the road at the worst possible moment. Gear selection is a faster and more reliable reference for cornering speed. A 2nd gear corner is slow and tight — hard braking, late apex, careful exit throttle. A 3rd gear corner is medium — moderate braking, standard line. A 4th gear corner is fast and sweeping — light braking or lift only, high commitment. Once you know the gear, you have a complete picture. Real world This is exactly how racing drivers describe corners to their engineers.

5
Apex kerb visibility — tight corner vs. long sweeper

The red-and-white painted apex kerb is one of the richest information sources on any circuit. When and how you first see it tells you the nature of the corner before you have arrived. If you can see the apex kerb early and clearly from a distance — tight, slow corner. Wait, use a very late apex, do not rush to the inside. If you cannot see the apex kerb yet — long, wide, sweeping corner that curves away from you. Be patient, do not turn in early, carry more speed. This single cue prevents the most common beginner mistake: turning in too early on a long corner and running wide on the exit. Sim Use cockpit or bumper cam — bonnet cam hides this completely.

The Combined Read — One Corner in Five Steps

Brake markers on the right → position left, left-hand turn coming. Distance boards present → hard braking corner. Apex kerb visible immediately → tight corner, wait for late apex. Target gear is 2nd → confirms slow corner. Kerbing begins on entry → corner has started, turn in now. None of that required any memorisation. All of it was read from the environment in real time. This is the difference between a driver who needs 30 laps to learn a track and one who is competitive in 5.

Chapter 03 · Driving Without Assists

Remove the safety net.
Find real speed.

Assists are training wheels. They hide the car's true behaviour and mask your inputs. Removing them feels terrifying at first, but every assist you remove reveals a new layer of the car — and a new layer of your own skill.

The Three Main Assists

ABS
Anti-lock Braking

With ABS on: The system prevents wheel lock automatically. You can apply maximum brake pressure without consequence.

With ABS off: You must threshold brake manually. Lock a wheel and you lose all steering control. The car goes straight, regardless of steering input.

How to train: Brake earlier than you think necessary. Progressively increase pressure until you feel the edge of lock, then ease fractionally back. That edge is your peak braking point.

Traction Control
TC

With TC on: The system cuts engine power when it detects wheelspin, preventing loss of traction on corner exit.

With TC off: You must roll onto the throttle carefully, especially in slow and medium-speed corners. Rear-wheel drive cars are particularly punishing — too much throttle too early causes snap oversteer.

How to train: Exit corners at 30–40% throttle initially, increasing the rate of application over multiple laps until you find the edge.

Stability Control
Most impactful

With SC on: The system counteracts slides automatically by braking individual wheels or reducing power without any input from you.

With SC off: You must countersteer every slide yourself. This is the hardest skill to develop but also the most rewarding — it is the difference between driving and truly feeling a car.

How to train: Drop AI difficulty significantly when first removing this. Expect slides. Learn to catch them. Gradually raise difficulty as your reactions improve.

The Step-Down Method

Never remove all assists simultaneously. Use this structured progression to remove one at a time, dropping AI difficulty at each step until comfortable before moving to the next.

Step 1: All assists on → learn the track fully, focus on racing line and brake points only.
Step 2: Remove stability control → lower AI difficulty by 10. Learn to feel the rear. Countersteer becomes muscle memory.
Step 3: Remove traction control → lower AI difficulty by 5–10. Focus on smooth throttle application on exit. Be patient with the power.
Step 4: Remove ABS → lower AI difficulty by 5. Trail your braking. Feel for the lock threshold. Shorter, harder braking zones.
Step 5: All assists off → raise AI difficulty gradually back to your baseline. You are now a significantly faster and more complete driver than when you started.

Force Feedback — Your Most Valuable Tool

Ironically, the most important tool in sim racing is the one that gets most overlooked. Force feedback is not an assist — it is information. Your steering wheel communicates what your tyres are experiencing, and learning to read that data is what separates fast drivers from slow ones.

FFB
What it tells you

Vibration through the wheel signals tyre grip loss. The wheel goes light when you are understeering. Sharp jolts indicate kerbs. Texture changes communicate road surface grip levels.

SET
Setting it correctly

Set FFB at a level where you feel detail clearly but the wheel does not overpower you. Over-heavy FFB causes fatigue and masks subtle information. Too light and you miss the early warning signs of grip loss.

Chapter 04 · Your First Race

Fast laps alone don't win races.
Race craft does.

You have learned car control, racing lines, and how to lap cleanly. The moment you are racing other humans, everything changes. Positioning, decisions, and mental composure matter as much as raw pace.

Overtaking — The 50/50 Rule

The fundamental rule of overtaking: if your car's nose is alongside the other car by the apex, you have a right to space on track. If it is not, you do not. Arriving late and fast with your nose behind the other car — the divebomb — means any contact is your responsibility.

In iRacing, ACC, and most rated platforms, incidents directly affect your Safety Rating. Protecting it early in your racing career is more important than gaining a single position through a risky move.

Defending

You are permitted one move to defend your position. Moving twice — the "chop" — is considered unsporting and is penalised in most regulated series. Cover the inside line before the braking zone. If your opponent chooses the outside, that is their decision to manage.

Starts, Strategy & Dirty Air

01
Race starts

The majority of first-lap incidents happen because of overcorrection and panic braking. Hold your position, stay calm, and watch the car directly ahead. Do not brake early out of fear — brake at your normal reference point.

02
Dirty air

The car ahead disrupts airflow, reducing your downforce. Following closely means your braking distances are longer and your cornering grip is reduced. Leave slightly more margin when following a car in the braking zone.

03
Tyre management

You do not need to push 100% every lap. If your position is safe, lift and coast into braking zones to preserve tyres. In longer races, the driver who manages tyres best almost always finishes ahead of the driver who pushes hardest.

The Mental Approach

Your first race will go wrong. Accept this before you start. Something unexpected will happen — a collision, a spin, a mistake. What separates improving drivers from stagnating ones is not whether mistakes happen, but what they do with the information afterwards.

Post-Race Review

After every race, ask three questions: Where did I lose the most time? Where did incidents occur and what caused them? What was my tyre state in the final laps? Answering these honestly turns every race into a coaching session, regardless of your finishing position.

Focus relentlessly on clean laps over fast laps. One fast lap surrounded by errors is worth far less than consistent, clean laps at 97% pace. Consistency is the foundation every fast driver builds their speed on top of — never the other way around.

Racing Flags & Signals

The universal language
of motorsport

Flags are the communication system between race control and drivers. They apply in F1, GT racing, rally, and most sim platforms. Know them without hesitation — in a real car or a sim, hesitating at a flag costs either a penalty or a crash.

Flag
Name
Meaning & Action Required
🟢
Green Flag
Session started or resumed. Track is clear. Racing is permitted. Full speed, no restrictions.
🟡
Yellow Flag
Hazard on track. Slow down significantly. No overtaking. Be prepared to take avoiding action. Single yellow = localised hazard.
🟡🟡
Double Yellow
Serious hazard. Track may be blocked. Slow down drastically. Be prepared to stop. Overtaking strictly prohibited.
🔴
Red Flag
Session stopped immediately. Return to pit lane at reduced speed. Do not race, do not push. Serious incident or dangerous conditions.
🔵
Blue Flag
Shown to a lapped car being approached by a lead lap car. Move aside within three blue flags or face a time penalty. The approaching car has right of way.
⬛
Black Flag
Disqualification. Return to pit lane immediately on the next lap. Shown with the car number. Most serious individual penalty in motorsport.
B/W
Black & White
Final warning for unsporting behaviour — blocking, weaving, or dangerous driving. Next offence will result in a penalty. One warning only.
⬛🟠
Meatball Flag
Black flag with orange circle. Your car has a mechanical defect or debris that poses a danger. Return to the pits on your next lap.
⬜
White Flag
Slow vehicle on track — safety car, recovery vehicle, or significantly damaged car. Proceed with caution, be prepared to encounter it.
⬛⬜
Chequered Flag
The race or session is finished. Slow down progressively. Do not make any sudden moves. Celebrate appropriately.
Flags in the Sim HUD

Most sims display flag indicators in your HUD or on the steering wheel display. Yellow sector indicators show precisely where on track the hazard is. A yellow in Sector 2 means only that sector requires slowing — not the whole lap. Read the sector, not just the flag colour.

Sim Skills in the Real World

The road is just another
circuit you haven't mapped yet

Sim racing genuinely teaches real-world driving skills. The physics are the same. The techniques are the same. What changes is the consequence of getting it wrong.

🤲
Smooth inputs

The sim trained you to be smooth. Apply this everywhere on the road. Smooth braking reduces stopping distances. Smooth steering prevents loss of control in wet conditions. Smooth acceleration reduces tyre wear. Every road driver benefits from this.

👁
Spatial awareness

Racing games train you to constantly monitor the positions of other cars. On public roads this translates to better mirror use, earlier hazard detection, and the habit of knowing what is around you at all times — not just what is ahead.

📍
Reference braking points

You trained yourself to brake at specific visual markers — a bridge, a sign, a line. This transfers directly to real driving. Pick consistent reference points on roads you drive regularly and your braking becomes more consistent, predictable, and safe.

🌧
Wet weather confidence

If you have driven a wet lap in ACC, you already understand the physics. Longer braking distances, gentler throttle application, wider, more patient lines. Drivers who have sim experience in wet conditions are typically far more composed in real rain than those who have not.

🔄
Tyre management mindset

Thinking about what your tyres are doing makes you a more conscious real-world driver. You start to feel when the front is washing wide or the rear feels light, and you adjust instinctively — rather than panicking or ignoring it entirely.

📐
Weight transfer awareness

Understanding that braking loads the front and acceleration loads the rear translates directly to how you navigate roundabouts, motorway exits, and tight junctions. You stop fighting the physics and start working with them.

⚠

The critical difference: In a sim, you reset. In a real car, you do not. Always apply a safety buffer when translating sim technique to real-world driving. The racing instincts are genuinely useful — but the public road is not a circuit. Other drivers are unpredictable, surfaces vary, and the consequences of a mistake are permanent. Use the knowledge, not the aggression.

Motorsport Terminology

Speak the language
of the paddock

These are the terms you will hear in F1 commentary, racing team radio, sim racing communities, and track day briefings. Know them and you will understand the sport at a deeper level.

Understeer
Front tyres lose grip — the car pushes wide of the intended line. Often called "push" in American motorsport. The front washes, the car goes straight regardless of steering.
Oversteer
Rear tyres lose grip — the rear steps out. The back end tries to overtake the front. Requires countersteering to correct. Can be gradual or a sudden snap.
Snap Oversteer
A sudden, violent rear slide with very little warning time. Often caused by cold tyres, excessive mid-corner speed, or aggressive trail throttle. Extremely hard to catch.
Lift-off Oversteer
Oversteer caused by suddenly releasing the throttle mid-corner. Weight shifts to the front, unloading the rear. Common in high-power rear-wheel drive cars.
Trail Braking
Gradually releasing brake pressure into the corner rather than finishing all braking before turn-in. Keeps weight on the front for rotation and improved corner-entry grip.
Dirty Air
Turbulent airflow behind a car that disrupts the aerodynamics of the following car. Reduces downforce and therefore grip for the car behind, making overtaking harder.
DRS
Drag Reduction System — opens a slot in the rear wing to reduce aerodynamic drag on straights. F1-specific. Available in designated zones when within 1 second of the car ahead.
ERS / KERS
Energy Recovery System — stores energy from braking and deploys it as electrical boost. F1-specific. Drivers manage deployment strategically throughout a lap.
Lock-up
Braking so hard a wheel stops rotating while the car is still moving. The tyre slides on the surface, creating a flat spot and a plume of tyre smoke. Destroys braking efficiency.
Graining
The tyre surface tears away in small rubber pieces due to thermal stress before the tyre is properly up to temperature. Creates a rough, grippy-then-slippery surface cycle.
Blistering
Deep tyre damage caused by sustained, excessive heat. Bubbles form under the tyre surface and burst, causing violent vibration and sudden grip loss.
Marbles
Small pieces of rubber shed by tyres that collect off-line, particularly in corners. The off-line surface has very low grip due to these rubber balls. Venturing off-line is dangerous.
Apex
The innermost point of a corner that a car clips when following the optimal racing line. Can be geometric (mid-corner) or late (after mid-corner) depending on the corner.
Delta
Your time relative to a reference lap — positive means slower, negative means faster. Under a Safety Car or VSC, drivers must maintain a specific delta time (not too fast, not too slow).
Gap
The time interval between you and the car ahead or behind, measured in seconds. Displayed on your pit wall board or team radio during the race.
Safety Car
Deployed when conditions are dangerous. Drivers must follow it and may not overtake. Bunches the field and often triggers strategic pit stop windows.
VSC
Virtual Safety Car — introduced in F1 after Jules Bianchi's accident in 2014. Drivers must maintain a prescribed delta time rather than physically following a car.
Box
Radio instruction meaning "come into the pit lane." The word "pit" is avoided on radio to prevent confusion with "split." Short, unambiguous, universal.
Sector Time
The track is divided into three sectors. Individual sector times reveal exactly where on track you are losing or gaining time versus your reference lap or opponent.
Safety Rating
iRacing-specific metric measuring how cleanly you race. Incidents reduce it, clean races increase it. Determines which license tier and series you can compete in.
