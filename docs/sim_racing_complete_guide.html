Chapter 01 · Car Physics

The car doesn’t lie.
Physics always wins.

Before you can be fast, understand what is happening underneath you. Every slide, every push, every wheelspin has a cause rooted in physics.

Weight Transfer — The Foundation

A car’s four tyres share the vehicle’s total weight. But that weight shifts with every input. A tyre carrying more weight has more grip. This is why the outside rear tyre does so much work in a corner — it’s under maximum load.

Understeer, Oversteer & Neutral Handling

US
Understeer — Front Slides Wide

Front tyres overwhelmed. The car goes straight regardless of steering angle. Causes: too much entry speed, too much throttle in FWD, front-heavy setup.

Fix: Ease throttle, wait for re-grip, use a later apex next lap.

OS
Oversteer — Rear Steps Out

Rear tyres lose traction. Requires countersteer. Causes: too much throttle on exit (RWD), braking into a corner, cold tyres, sudden lift-off mid-corner.

Fix: Countersteer smoothly. Never lift suddenly — it worsens it.

NE
Neutral — The Goal

Neither end dominates. Car rotates cleanly. Achieved through correct inputs, proper setup, and understanding the traction budget at all four corners.

Feel for: The car going exactly where you point it.

Drivetrain matters

RWD is most prone to oversteer — rear wheels drive AND provide lateral grip. FWD understeers under power — fronts doing everything. AWD is most stable but snaps without warning at the limit.

Snap Oversteer vs Gradual Oversteer

Gradual oversteer builds progressively — you feel it coming and have time to catch it. Snap oversteer happens almost instantly — cold tyre, sudden weight shift, bumpy corner. By the time your brain registers it, it’s already too far gone.

Real World

If the rear breaks loose on a wet road, do not lift suddenly — this transfers weight forward, further unloading the rear. Ease off smoothly and steer gently into the slide. This is counterintuitive but correct.

Chapter 02 · The Traction Budget

Every tyre has exactly
100% of grip to spend.

The single most important mental model in driving. Understand it and every input decision becomes logical.

The Friction Circle

Each tyre can provide a maximum total force — picture a circle. Braking uses one direction. Cornering uses another. Acceleration uses another. You can combine forces, but never exceed the circle’s edge. Use 90% on braking and only 10% remains for turning.

The Golden Sequence

Brake fully → release brake → turn in → unwind wheel → apply throttle. Each action gets the tyre’s full attention. This is physics, not preference.

Threshold Braking & Trail Braking

Peak braking force occurs at the exact moment just before wheel lock. Brake any harder and you lock up — losing steering and increasing stopping distance. Brake less and you leave performance unused.

Trail Braking

Gradually releasing brake pressure as you turn in keeps weight on the front tyres through corner entry — improving rotation and reducing understeer. Used by every professional racing driver. Also genuinely useful on real roads in emergency situations.

Chapter 03 · The Racing Line

The fastest path is never
the shortest path.

The racing line maximises corner radius — a larger radius allows higher speed without exceeding the traction budget.

Out — In — Out

Start wide, cut to the inside at the apex, track back out wide. You turn the corner into the largest possible arc. Larger arc = less steering angle = more speed through the corner.

OUT
Wide Entry

Position on the outside before the corner. This sets up the maximum possible arc and gives you the longest run-up to the apex. Don’t move toward the inside yet.

IN
Apex — Cut Inside

The innermost point you clip. Late apex — hitting the inside kerb past the geometric midpoint — is almost always faster because it straightens the exit sooner.

OUT
Wide Exit — Track Out

Let the car drift back to the outside as you unwind the wheel and apply progressive throttle. Use every centimetre of tarmac available to you.

The beginner trap

Most beginners lose time on exit, not entry. Turning in too early forces a lift on exit — killing all the time gained. Always sacrifice entry to optimise exit, especially before a long straight.

Corner Combinations

1
Chicane — sacrifice the first, own the second

The exit of the second element leads onto the straight. Accept a slightly slower entry for a cleaner, earlier-throttle exit. Exit speed = straight speed

2
Double Apex — two clips, one corner

Stay wide through the first half, then cut cleanly to the second apex for a straight exit. Rushing the first apex ruins the second. Eau Rouge, Copse

3
Hairpin — latest possible apex

Tightest corners, longest straights. Late apex means earlier throttle and maximum exit speed. Brake deep, turn in late, be patient with power. Power out early = wins races

Chapter 04 · Reading the Track

5 visual cues that replace
memorisation entirely.

Every circuit tells you what it needs. Read the environment in real time and drive an unfamiliar track confidently from lap one.

1
Brake markers — which side of the track to be on

100m boards on the left = right-hand corner coming. Boards on the right = left-hand corner. Before you even see the corner, the boards are already telling you where to position. Cockpit cam in simReal track days too

2
Kerbing — corners announce themselves

Sudden kerbing appearing on a straight means a corner is arriving. Begin preparation immediately: position wide, start trail braking, locate your turn-in point. The kerb gives you advance notice. All simsIdentical on real circuits

3
Braking requirement — hard, lift, or flat?

Distance boards present = hard braking. No boards but track compression = lift or feather. No boards, flat entry = commit fully. Correctly identifying these three saves enormous amounts of lap time. FFB confirms over-braking

4
Gear selection — your instant corner descriptor

2nd gear = slow, tight, hard braking, late apex. 3rd gear = medium speed, standard line. 4th gear = fast sweeper, light brake or lift only. Know the gear and you have the whole picture. How drivers brief engineers

5
Apex kerb visibility — wait or commit?

Can see the apex kerb early = tight corner, wait for late apex. Cannot see it = long sweeper, carry speed. Prevents the most common beginner mistake — turning in too early. Cockpit or bumper cam

Combined Read — An Example

Brake markers on the right → left-hander. Distance boards present → hard braking. Apex kerb visible early → tight corner, late apex. 2nd gear → confirms it’s slow. Kerbing begins on entry → turn in now. None of that required memorisation. All read from the environment in real time.

Chapter 05 · Driving Without Assists

Remove the safety net.
Find real speed.

Assists hide the car’s true behaviour. Removing them systematically reveals a faster, more complete driver.

The Three Core Assists

ABS
Anti-lock Braking

On: Maximum brake pressure without locking. System modulates automatically.

Off: You must threshold brake manually. Lock a wheel and you lose steering entirely.

Learn it: Brake earlier. Slowly increase pressure to the onset of lock, then back off fractionally. That edge is your new brake point.

Traction Control
TC

On: Throttle cut when wheels spin. You can be aggressive on corner exit without consequence.

Off: RWD cars snap oversteerly if you apply full throttle before the car is pointing straight. You feel the rear squirm and must modulate.

Learn it: Exit corners at 30–40% throttle. Build progressively over many laps as you feel the limit.

Stability Control
Hardest to remove

On: Individual brakes applied automatically to counteract slides. Car straightens itself.

Off: Every slide needs manual countersteer. Hardest skill, biggest reward.

Learn it: Lower AI significantly. Expect spins. Each spin teaches you where the limit is.

The Step-Down Method

Never strip all assists at once. Build each neural pathway one at a time:

All assists on — learn the track layout. Focus only on racing line and brake points. Don’t try to do everything at once.
Remove Stability Control — lower AI by 10. Start feeling the rear. Countersteering becomes muscle memory over 20–30 laps.
Remove Traction Control — lower AI by another 5–10. Be patient with throttle on exit. Feel the tyres at their limit.
Remove ABS — lower AI by 5. Trail brake release. Feel for the onset of lock. Brake earlier, build pressure back up.
All assists off — you are now feeling everything the car feels. Raise AI gradually. You will be measurably faster.

Force Feedback — Your Information Channel

FFB is not an assist — it is data. The wheel goes light when the front understeers. It vibrates when tyres are near the limit. It jolts over kerbs and changes texture over different surfaces. Set it where you feel detail clearly without it overpowering you.

Chapter 06 · Race Craft

Fast laps alone
don’t win races.

The moment you race other humans, raw pace is only one factor. Positioning, composure, and tyre management matter equally.

Overtaking — The Core Rules

The 50% rule: if your nose is level with the other driver’s cockpit by the apex, you have a right to track position. If you’re not there, you don’t. Any contact at that point is your responsibility.

Defending

One defensive move only. Moving twice is a “chop” — penalised in most series. Cover the inside before the braking zone. If they go outside, that is their choice and their risk to manage.

The Divebomb Trap

Late, aggressive braking moves look spectacular but usually overshoot the corner, cause contact, or result in a penalty. A calculated undercut on the next lap is almost always better strategy than a risky Pass at Turn 1.

What Actually Wins Races — Priority Ranked

01
Consistency over single fast laps
A driver doing 1:32.4 every lap beats one doing 1:31.8 then 1:33.5 then making an error. Variance is the enemy. Smooth, repeatable laps compound over race distance.
02
Tyre management — especially the final stint
Tyres degrade. Drivers who manage them have more grip when it matters most. Lift and coast in safe positions. Every unnecessary heavy-braking corner costs tyre life that compounds.
03
First lap composure — survive Turn 1
Most incidents happen on the opening lap. Panic braking causes chain reactions. Brake at your normal reference point, hold your line, and let the chaos sort itself out around you.
04
Dirty air management
Following close disrupts your front downforce and increases braking distances. Leave a 1–1.5 second gap when following closely to maintain aerodynamic performance and tyre health.

Post-Race Analysis — How You Actually Improve

?
Where did I lose time?

Use sector times. If Sector 2 is consistently 0.3s off pace, that’s your focus. Don’t guess — the data tells you exactly.

?
Where did incidents happen?

Repeat incidents at the same corner mean a technique issue, not bad luck. Identify it, name it, approach it differently next session.

?
What was my tyre state in the final laps?

Significantly slower in the last third = you overdrove the tyres early. Pace left = too conservative. Find the balance through data, not guesswork.

Chapter 07 · Racing Flags

The universal language
of motorsport.

Know them without hesitation — hesitating costs a penalty or a crash.

Green Flag
Session started or resumed. Track clear. Full racing speed, no restrictions.
Yellow Flag
Hazard on track in this sector. Slow down. No overtaking. Be prepared for avoiding action.
Double Yellow
Serious hazard — track may be blocked. Slow drastically. Prepare to stop. No overtaking under any circumstances.
Red Flag
Session stopped. Return to pit lane at reduced speed. Do not race. Serious incident or dangerous conditions on track.
Blue Flag
You are a lapped car. A lead-lap car is approaching. Move aside within three blue flags or receive a time penalty.
Black Flag
Disqualification. Return to pits immediately. Shown with your car number. Most severe individual penalty in motorsport.
Black & White Flag
Final warning for unsporting behaviour — weaving, blocking, dangerous driving. Next offence results in a penalty.
Meatball Flag (Black + Orange circle)
Your car has a mechanical defect or is shedding debris. Return to the pits on your next lap for inspection.
White Flag
Slow vehicle on track ahead — safety car, recovery truck, or heavily damaged car. Proceed with caution.
Chequered Flag
Race or session finished. Slow progressively. Do not make sudden moves. The result is now decided.
Safety Car (Yellow + SC board)
Safety car deployed. Maintain your delta time. No overtaking. Gaps close — creates strategic pit stop windows.
In the Sim HUD

Most sims display flag indicators per sector. A yellow in Sector 2 means the hazard is there specifically — not the whole circuit. Read the sector indicator, not just the colour.

Chapter 08 · Terminology

Speak the language
of the paddock.

Terms from F1 commentary, team radio, sim communities, and real track day briefings. Hover terms marked › for a deeper explanation.

Understeer ›
Front tyres lose grip — car pushes wide regardless of steering angle.
By drivetrain

FWD: Most prone. Fronts steer, drive, and brake at once. Easing throttle often restores grip.

RWD: Less prone. Usually means entry speed too high or nose-heavy setup.

AWD: Moderate. Under power, fronts can exceed grip limit. Hard to correct once established.

Oversteer ›
Rear tyres lose grip — rear slides out. Requires countersteering to correct.
By drivetrain

RWD: Most prone. Too much throttle = rear breaks away. Classic drift. Lift-off oversteer also pronounced.

FWD: Rarely under power. Can oversteer on sudden lift-off — some drivers use this deliberately to rotate.

AWD: Least prone but most dangerous when it occurs — arrives suddenly without warning.

Snap Oversteer ›
Sudden, violent rear slide with very little warning. Cold tyres or combined braking + steering.
Why it matters

Snap rotates the car faster than human reaction time in some cases. The brain takes ~150ms to register and respond. At speed, that is several metres of uncontrolled rotation.

Prevention: Warm tyres properly, smooth inputs, never combine hard braking with full steering lock.

Trail Braking
Gradually releasing brake into corner entry to keep weight on the front for rotation and grip.
Lift-off Oversteer
Releasing throttle mid-corner transfers weight forward, unloading the rear which swings out.
Lock-up
Braking so hard a wheel stops rotating while the car moves. Flat spot + complete loss of steering.
Late Apex
Turning in later, hitting the inside kerb past the geometric midpoint. Allows earlier throttle on exit.
Dirty Air
Turbulent airflow behind a car that reduces the following car’s downforce and increases braking distances.
DRS
Drag Reduction System — opens rear wing flap to reduce drag on straights. F1 only. Within 1s of car ahead.
ERS / KERS
Energy Recovery System — harvests braking energy and deploys it as an electrical power boost.
Graining
Tyre surface tears before reaching operating temperature. Creates rubber pellets that reduce grip. Cold-condition problem.
Blistering
Deep tyre damage from sustained excessive heat. Bubbles form under the compound surface. Causes sudden grip loss.
Marbles
Rubber debris shed by tyres that collects off the racing line. Off-line grip dramatically reduced.
Apex
The innermost point a car clips on the racing line. Can be geometric (midpoint) or late (past midpoint).
Delta Time
Your time relative to a reference lap. Positive = slower. Negative = faster. Also the target under VSC.
Undercut
Pit before your rival, set faster laps on fresh tyres, emerge ahead when they pit. Track position via strategy.
Overcut
Stay out longer than your rival, build a gap, emerge ahead when they pit. Counter to the undercut.
VSC
Virtual Safety Car — drivers maintain a prescribed delta time. Gaps remain unchanged, unlike physical SC.
Box
Radio code for “pit in.” The word “pit” is avoided as it sounds similar to “split” on team radio.
Traction Circle
Visual model of a tyre’s total grip capacity. Any combination of forces must stay within the circle’s edge.
Sector Time
Track split into three sectors. Individual times reveal exactly where you gain or lose relative to a reference.
Flat Spot
Tyre worn flat from a lock-up. Causes vibration at speed and reduced braking effectiveness. Irreversible without a stop.
Safety Rating
iRacing metric measuring how cleanly you race. Determines your licence tier and which series you can enter.
Traction Circle
Model showing a tyre’s total grip. Combined braking and cornering forces must not exceed the circle boundary.

Chapter 09 · Why Cars Need Different Tuning

Same settings,
completely different result.

A tune that makes an F1 car faster will make a muscle car dangerous. Every tuning decision depends on drivetrain layout, weight distribution, and power delivery. Understand this before touching any setting.

The Three Drivetrain Personalities

RWD
Rear-Wheel Drive

The rear wheels must drive the car forward AND provide lateral cornering grip simultaneously. This fundamental conflict is the source of oversteer.

Tuning priority: Rear grip and stability first. Too stiff at rear = snap oversteer. Too soft = understeer on throttle.

Stiffer rear ARBMore rear downforceRear diff lockingAvoid loose rear setup

Examples: Ferrari 488, BMW M3, Porsche 911, Nissan 370Z

FWD
Front-Wheel Drive

The fronts must steer, drive, and brake all at once. This is why FWD cars understeer under power — the front tyres are overloaded with too many demands.

Tuning priority: Reduce understeer. Achieve rotation on entry so the fronts can focus on driving the car out.

Softer front ARBMore front camberAvoid stiff front setup

Examples: Honda Civic Type R, VW Golf GTI, Renault Megane RS

AWD
All-Wheel Drive

Power to all four wheels. Most stable platform but least communicative. AWD hides the limit then reaches it suddenly. Torque split between front and rear changes everything.

Tuning priority: Balance the torque split. Too much front = understeer. Too much rear = snap oversteer.

Adjust torque splitCentre diff criticalDon’t trust the stability

Examples: Subaru WRX STI, Mitsubishi EVO, Audi R8

Weight Distribution — The Hidden Variable

Where the engine sits determines the car’s natural balance before any tuning. This is why two RWD cars can behave completely differently from each other.

FR
Front-Engine, Rear-Drive

Most common RWD layout. Weight over the front axle, rear is lighter. Prone to oversteer under power. BMW M cars, Mustang, most sports cars.

MR
Mid-Engine, Rear-Drive

Near-perfect 50:50 weight distribution. Naturally balanced but rotates very quickly. Requires careful ARB and diff tuning. Ferrari 458, McLaren 720S, Lamborghini Huracán.

RR
Rear-Engine, Rear-Drive

Engine behind the rear axle creates a pendulum effect — once the rear starts rotating, it keeps going. Porsche 911. Requires very conservative setup and smooth inputs.

FF
Front-Engine, Front-Drive

Engine over front wheels means nose-heavy balance. Fronts doing everything. Natural understeer tendency requires soft front ARB, negative camber, and careful diff setup.

The key principle

Every tuning decision aims to move handling toward neutral — neither pushing the front (understeer) nor swinging the rear (oversteer). Your starting point depends entirely on the car’s architecture. Understanding where you start is half the tuning job.

Power Delivery Changes Everything Too

High-torque, low-revving engines (V8 muscle cars, diesels) produce peak grip demand instantly. These cars need stiffer, more planted setups to absorb sudden torque spikes without the rear stepping out.

High-revving, low-torque engines build power progressively. The car has time to settle into acceleration. Softer, more reactive setups work better — the driver can modulate more precisely.

Turbocharged engines have power lag followed by a surge. The setup must account for the car’s behaviour both before boost arrives and after. Differential preload and traction control mapping become especially important.

Chapter 10 · Tyres & Pressures

Start here. Always.

Tyre pressure affects grip, wear rate, temperature, and handling balance. The fastest, cheapest adjustment you can make. Get this right before touching anything else.

Tyre Pressure
Front Tyre Pressure
Controls front grip, temperature, and wear pattern
Grip▼

Too low = tyre rolls onto sidewall, vague feel, edge wears faster. Too high = only centre contacts road, middle overheats quickly and wears. Both reduce grip.

Pressure28 PSI
18 PSI — Too low, vague38 PSI — Too high, overheats
Increase pressure when

Centre of tyre cold, edges overheating. Car feels nervous. Tyre rolling onto sidewall under hard cornering.

Decrease pressure when

Centre overheating, edges cold. Car feels vague and imprecise. Contact patch too small.

RWD: Slightly higher front pressure — front only steers and brakes, not driving. FWD: Lower front pressure to maximise contact patch for the overworked front tyres. AWD: Balanced pressures, within 1–2 PSI front to rear.
Rear Tyre Pressure
Controls rear grip, traction, and stability
Grip▼

High rear pressure reduces contact patch — less traction for RWD, more oversteer tendency. Low rear pressure gives more contact patch and grip, but the tyre can overheat under sustained heavy load.

Increase pressure when

Rear too grippy causing exit understeer. Rear degrading slower than fronts — uneven wear pattern.

Decrease pressure when

Rear losing grip or overheating mid-race. Rear degrading significantly faster than fronts.

RWD: Lower rear pressure = more contact patch = more traction. Critical for high-powered cars. FWD: Lower rear pressure allows rear to flex slightly, actually reducing understeer tendency. AWD: Match front and rear pressures closely.
Tyre Compound Selection
Softer = more peak grip, faster degradation. Harder = less grip, longer life.
Grip▼

Soft compound: Most pliable rubber, highest peak grip, reaches temperature quickly, degrades fastest. Good for qualifying laps or short race stints.

Medium compound: The balance point. Consistent over race distance. Default choice for most race setups and conditions.

Hard compound: Resists heat and wear. Takes many laps to warm up — you will feel understeer and poor grip until temperature builds. Ideal for long stints or hot track conditions.

Heavy, high-powered cars generate more tyre heat through mass and power — often need a harder compound than expected. Lightweight cars struggle to generate tyre heat and often need softer compounds even in warm conditions.
Chapter 11 · Gearing

Put your power
where you need it.

Gearing determines where in the rev range you operate on each section of track. Wrong gearing means running out of revs before the braking zone or spending time in the engine’s dead zone.

Final Drive Ratio
Master control — affects all gears simultaneously
Engine▼

A shorter (higher number) final drive compresses the entire gear range — the car hits each gear’s rev limit sooner. A longer (lower number) final drive spreads the range wider — each gear covers more speed before the next shift.

Shorter (higher number)

Better acceleration. Hits rev limit sooner. Better for tight tracks. Risk: hitting rev limit before the braking zone on long straights.

Longer (lower number)

Better top speed. More speed per gear. Better for high-speed circuits. Risk: sluggish corner exit if engine drops below power band.

High-torque engines can run longer final drives and still pull hard. High-revving low-torque engines need shorter final drives to stay in their narrow power band. Match the final drive to your slowest corner — that corner should put the car in its peak torque zone on exit.
Individual Gear Ratios (1st–6th)
Fine-tune where each gear peaks and hands off
Engine▼

Goal: every shift point should occur near peak power. 1st: Always short, only for launch. Middle gears (2nd–4th): Your corner-exit gears — tune so your most important slow corner puts you in the mid-power-band on exit. Top gear: Should reach maximum circuit speed slightly before the braking zone, not right at it.

Turbocharged: Keep gears shorter and closer together. Stay above the turbo spool speed — dropping too low means waiting for boost to build, losing time. Naturally aspirated: Wider spread is acceptable since power delivery is linear across the rev range.
Chapter 12 · Suspension

The car’s connection
to the road.

Suspension controls how the car reacts to bumps, weight transfer, and cornering forces. The most complex tuning area — small changes have large effects on balance.

Springs
Spring Rate — Front
Controls front dive under braking and front roll in corners
Balance▼

Stiff front springs = less dive under braking, less body roll, sharper turn-in. But less compliance over bumps — the front can lose contact over crests and rough sections.

Stiffer front springs

Reduces understeer. Better high-speed stability. Sharper turn-in. Can cause bouncing on rough tracks and loss of grip over bumps.

Softer front springs

More understeer. More compliance over bumps, better traction on rough tracks. Car feels lazier on initial turn-in.

FWD: Stiffer front springs reduce understeer — front compresses less and maintains contact patch better. High-downforce: Can run very stiff springs because aero provides the compliance springs normally would.
Spring Rate — Rear
Controls rear squat under acceleration and rear roll
Balance▼

Stiffer rear = less squat = more stability under power. But rear can bounce on bumps and lose traction intermittently. Softer rear = rear tyre stays planted over bumps better, but too soft means excessive weight transfer causing oversteer.

Stiffer rear springs

Reduces oversteer tendency. More rear stability under power. Better for smooth tracks. Risk: rear skipping on rough surfaces.

Softer rear springs

Better grip over bumpy surfaces. Tyre follows road better. Too soft causes oversteer as rear squats excessively under power.

RWD critical: Too stiff = snap oversteer on bumpy corners. Too soft = unpredictable under heavy braking. Aim for settled but compliant. FWD: Softer rear lets the rear swing slightly on entry — useful for rotation.
Dampers (Shock Absorbers)
Bump Damping — Front & Rear
Controls how fast the suspension compresses over a bump
Mechanical▼

Too stiff: Suspension cannot compress quickly enough over bumps. Tyre bounces rather than following the road. You lose contact patch and grip. Too soft: Suspension dives too easily. Weight transfer happens suddenly. Car feels loose and unpredictable under hard inputs.

Start bump damping at 60–70% of the maximum available range. Adjust only after springs are set correctly — dampers fine-tune the spring’s behaviour, not the overall stiffness direction.
Rebound Damping — Front & Rear
Controls how fast the suspension extends after compression
Mechanical▼

Too stiff: Suspension is slow to return after compression. Car gets “stuck” in a compressed state, especially in chicanes and fast direction changes. Feels locked down. Too soft: Car bounces back too quickly — unsettled over a series of bumps.

Rebound is typically set higher than bump damping. A common starting ratio is 6–7 bump : 9–10 rebound (on a scale of 1–10). High-downforce cars need higher rebound to control spring motion at high speed.
Ride Height
Ride Height — Front & Rear
Affects aero, centre of gravity, and ground clearance
Aero▼

Lower ride height reduces the centre of gravity (less body roll, more stable cornering) and on aerodynamic cars increases the venturi effect under the floor — more downforce. But too low = bottoming out over bumps. Rake angle (rear higher than front) pushes aerodynamic balance toward the front — more front downforce. Most race cars run slight rear-high rake.

High-downforce cars: Even 2mm ride height change shifts aero balance significantly. Must be measured precisely. Road and muscle cars: Ride height mainly affects CoG — lower is generally better until you start bottoming out on bumps.
Chapter 13 · Wheel Alignment

Angle the tyre.
Change the grip.

How the tyre sits relative to the road determines its contact patch shape. Among the highest-impact, most misunderstood tuning parameters available to you.

Camber — Front
Tyre tilt inward (negative) or outward (positive) viewed from front
Grip▼

When cornering, outside tyres tilt outward due to body roll. Negative camber pre-tilts the tyre inward so when the car rolls in a corner, the tyre becomes more upright — maximising contact patch and cornering grip. Too much negative camber = inner edge wears fast on straights and braking grip is reduced.

Front Camber-2.5°
-5.0° — Extreme, inner wear0° — No camber
FWD: More front negative camber (−2.5° to −3.5°) — front does everything and needs maximum cornering grip. RWD: −1.5° to −2.5° typical. Too much sacrifices straight-line braking stability. High-downforce: Less camber needed — aero pushes the tyre flatter in corners anyway.
Camber — Rear
Rear tyre tilt — affects cornering grip and traction balance
Grip▼

Rear camber works the same as front but the rear also needs to put power down in RWD/AWD cars. Too much negative camber reduces the contact patch for straight-line traction. Rear camber is typically less negative than front.

RWD: Keep conservative at −1.0° to −1.5°. Too much loses traction. FWD: −0.5° to −1.0° helps cornering stability without sacrificing anything. AWD: Match front and rear more closely — typically −1.5° to −2.5° front, −1.0° to −1.5° rear.
Toe — Front
Tyres angled inward (toe-in) or outward (toe-out) viewed from above
Balance▼

Toe-out: More responsive, sharper turn-in — each tyre already angled toward the corner. But creates instability at high speed. Toe-in: More stable, slower turn-in. Better for high-speed circuits. Most road cars run slight toe-in for safety.

FWD performance: Small front toe-out helps rotation — critical for reducing understeer. High-speed circuits: Front toe-in for stability on long straights.
Toe — Rear
Major stability vs rotation trade-off
Balance▼

Rear toe-in (standard): creates a self-correcting effect. If the rear starts to slide, the rear tyres angle to resist the slide. More stability, less rotation. Rear toe-out: Increases rotation dramatically. Can cause high-speed instability. Rarely used except in drift setups.

RWD: Rear toe-in almost always better — controls oversteer tendency. 0.1° to 0.2° is typical. FWD: Slight rear toe-in adds stability and reduces rear swing on lift-off. Both: Never go to rear toe-out unless you specifically want rotation at the cost of stability.
Caster Angle
Steering axis tilt — affects self-centering and dynamic camber
Balance▼

Higher positive caster creates more self-centering force — the steering naturally wants to return to straight ahead. Also adds dynamic negative camber when steering is applied, improving cornering grip. Requires more steering effort but gives better high-speed stability and feedback.

Most performance cars benefit from higher positive caster (5°–7° or more). In sim racing, higher caster directly improves force feedback quality — you feel the tyres more clearly through the steering wheel.
Chapter 14 · Anti-Roll Bars

Control the roll.
Dial the balance.

Anti-roll bars resist the car rolling in corners. Critically, they affect handling balance independently of spring rates — and their effect is counterintuitive.

The counterintuitive truth

A stiffer ARB increases load transfer at that axle. The axle with the stiffer ARB has less total cornering traction because the inside tyre unloads more aggressively. Stiffer front ARB = more understeer. Stiffer rear ARB = more oversteer. This is counterintuitive but correct.

Front Anti-Roll Bar Stiffness
Controls front lateral load transfer — understeer/oversteer balance lever
Balance▼

Stiffening the front ARB increases load transfer at the front axle. The inside front tyre unloads more aggressively. The front axle as a whole has less total cornering traction — resulting in more understeer. This is counterintuitive but proven.

Stiffer front ARB

More understeer. Better flat cornering (less body roll). Car resists rotation. More stable but pushes wide.

Softer front ARB

Less understeer. Front rotates more freely. More body roll. Better on bumpy tracks. Can create oversteer if rear ARB is much stiffer.

FWD: Softer front ARB is the primary understeer fix — front axle has more traction when load transfer is gentler. This single change makes the biggest difference in FWD handling. RWD: A stiffer front ARB than rear helps stability and reduces aggressive rotation on corner entry.
Rear Anti-Roll Bar Stiffness
Controls rear lateral load transfer — major oversteer lever
Balance▼

Stiffer rear ARB increases load transfer at the rear axle — inside rear unloads more, rear has less total cornering traction. Result: more oversteer tendency.

Stiffer rear ARB

More oversteer. Rear rotates more freely. Car feels agile. Risk: rear steps out under power or mid-corner.

Softer rear ARB

Less oversteer. Rear is more planted. Better traction on corner exit for RWD. Safer overall. More understeer tendency.

RWD: This is your primary oversteer control. Rear stepping out too much? Soften rear ARB before touching anything else. FWD: Stiffer rear ARB can help corner-entry rotation — unloading the rear lets the car pivot. AWD: Typically rear ARB slightly stiffer than front to prevent torque understeer.
Chapter 15 · Brakes

Balance the stop.
Control the entry.

Brake balance determines which end slows more aggressively. One of the few settings drivers adjust live during a race from the steering wheel.

Brake Balance (Bias) — Front / Rear
Proportion of braking force applied to front vs rear axle
Balance▼

Under hard braking, weight transfers to the front — the front tyres have more grip and can take more braking force. Most race cars run 55–65% front bias. Too much front: Rear brakes contribute little and front overheats on long braking zones. Too much rear: Rear locks easily (it’s light during braking), causing rotation and spin.

Brake Bias60% Front
50% Front / 50% Rear75% Front / 25% Rear
High-downforce cars: More rear bias than road cars — aero generates rear downforce giving rear tyres more grip under braking. Rear-engine cars (911): Can run more rear bias. FWD: Higher front bias — weight and drive are both at the front.
Brake Pressure (Force)
Overall maximum brake force multiplier
Mechanical▼

Higher pressure = more stopping force but easier lock-up without ABS. Lower pressure = more modulation range but may not achieve peak braking performance under maximum demand.

With ABS on, higher brake pressure is almost always better — ABS handles lock prevention. Without ABS, find the pressure where you can feel the threshold clearly. Heavier cars need higher pressure. Lightweight cars with large brake packages may benefit from lower pressure for better modulation feel.
Chapter 16 · Aerodynamics

Press the car down.
Pay with drag.

Aerodynamics only matter on cars generating meaningful downforce. For most road cars, skip this entirely and focus on mechanical grip instead.

Front Downforce / Front Wing Angle
Front grip at high speed at the cost of straight-line speed
Aero▼

More front wing angle = more front downforce = less understeer at high speed = more drag = lower top speed. Front and rear downforce must be balanced — running maximum front with minimum rear creates high-speed oversteer.

More front wing

Less high-speed understeer. Better fast sweepers. More braking stability. Costs top speed. Critical at Silverstone, Spa.

Less front wing

Higher top speed. More high-speed understeer. Better for Monza. Rear aerodynamically dominant.

Rear Downforce / Rear Wing Angle
Rear stability and RWD traction vs drag trade-off
Aero▼

Rear wing creates downforce over the rear axle — more traction for RWD at high speed and better high-speed stability. The largest drag-producing component on most race cars.

More rear wing

More rear grip at speed. Better stability. Improved RWD traction on corner exits. Significant top speed loss.

Less rear wing

Higher top speed. Rear feels light at speed. More oversteer in fast corners. Used at low-drag circuits: Monza, Le Mans.

RWD at high speed: Without sufficient rear downforce, the rear becomes light under acceleration and snaps into oversteer. This is why F1 cars run massive wings at Monaco and tiny wings at Monza — the circuit dictates the aero level, not personal preference.
No meaningful aero on your car?

Most muscle cars, touring cars, and early sports cars generate negligible aerodynamic downforce. Skip aero tuning entirely for these cars and focus on mechanical grip: springs, dampers, ARBs, camber, and tyres. That is where all the performance lives.

Chapter 17 · Differential

The most misunderstood
tuning parameter.

The differential controls how torque is shared between the two driven wheels on the same axle. The single most impactful parameter for corner exit behaviour.

What a differential does

In a corner, the inside wheel travels a shorter distance than the outside wheel. Without a differential, both wheels would be locked together — the inner would scrub (creating understeer) or the outer would spin. A differential allows each wheel to rotate at different speeds. The tuning question: how much should it resist this speed difference?

Acceleration Lock (Power-On Differential)
How locked the diff is when you apply throttle
Grip▼

High lock (closed diff): Both wheels forced to turn together on throttle. Inside tyre fights the corner — creates understeer on throttle. But more traction: both tyres contribute. Good for tight, slow corners where maximum traction matters. Low lock (open diff): On slippery surface or mid-corner, power goes to the least resistant wheel (usually inside, which is unloaded) — wheelspin on inside, less overall traction.

More locking

More traction on exit. Understeer on throttle. Car pushes wide when accelerating. Stable and consistent. Good for novices.

Less locking

More oversteer on exit. Rear can step out under power. More rotation. Experienced drivers can steer with the throttle.

RWD critical: Enough locking prevents inside wheel spinning uselessly — but not so much that exit understeer prevents early full throttle. FWD: Acceleration locking helps spread torque evenly, reducing the snap toward the spin side. AWD: This plus the centre diff split together determine exit character entirely.
Deceleration Lock (Brake-Off Differential)
How locked the diff is when you lift or brake
Balance▼

High decel lock: More engine braking effect on turn-in. The rear can rotate more under braking as both rear wheels are slowed together. Useful for inducing rotation. Can cause oversteer on entry. Low decel lock: Less engine braking through the diff. Less rotation on turn-in — more understeer on entry. Safer, more predictable corner entry behaviour.

Classic RWD tune: Low decel lock + medium acceleration lock = stable entry with controlled exit. The low decel lock gives you a clean, predictable turn-in, and the acceleration lock gives enough traction to prevent inside wheelspin on exit.
Differential Preload
Minimum torque required to allow wheels to rotate at different speeds
Mechanical▼

Preload is a baseline resistance built into the differential — it takes this much torque to allow any speed difference between wheels. The diff is always partially locked regardless of throttle position. Higher preload: Always slightly locked, more consistent and predictable, but always creates slight understeer. Lower preload: More responsive to throttle and brake inputs, sharper handling, but less consistent across different throttle positions.

Beginners: higher preload is more forgiving. Experienced drivers: lower preload enables throttle-steering — adjusting the car’s angle with fine throttle inputs. This is how professional drivers “steer with the throttle” in RWD cars at the limit.

Problem Solver · What to Change When

Feel the problem.
Find the fix.

You’re on track, something feels wrong. Here are the most common handling problems and what to change first — in order of impact.

Car pushes wide (understeer) in the middle of corners
▼

Classic mid-corner understeer — front tyres overloaded. Try in this order:

→
Reduce front tyre pressure by 1–2 PSI to increase contact patch
→
Add more front negative camber (e.g. −2.0° to −2.5°)
→
Soften the front anti-roll bar — less load transfer at front axle = more front traction
→
Stiffen the rear anti-roll bar — more rear load transfer helps the car rotate
→
Reduce front spring rate slightly — more compliance, better tyre contact over bumps
→
Driving tip: Use more trail braking to keep weight on the front through corner entry
Rear steps out on corner exit — power oversteer (RWD)
▼

Rear losing traction under acceleration. Try in this order:

→
Increase acceleration diff lock — prevents inside wheel spinning uselessly
→
Reduce rear tyre pressure by 1 PSI — more contact patch, more traction
→
Soften the rear anti-roll bar — less load transfer, rear has more traction
→
Add more rear downforce (if car has aero) — pushes rear into the road
→
Soften rear springs — better compliance, rear stays planted over bumps
→
Driving tip: Be more patient with throttle — wait until the car is pointing toward the exit
Car snaps into oversteer suddenly on corner entry under braking
▼

Entry snap — rear unloading too quickly under combined braking and turning:

→
Increase front brake bias — less rear braking = rear doesn’t lock as easily on entry
→
Reduce deceleration diff lock — less engine braking through diff = less rotation on entry
→
Add rear toe-in — stabilises the rear under combined braking and turning loads
→
Soften rear ARB — rear resists rotation better when it has more traction
→
Reduce rear spring rate — more compliant under combined braking and steering
→
Driving tip: Release brake more progressively on turn-in to reduce sudden weight transfer
Car feels nervous and unstable at high speed on straights
▼

High-speed instability — aerodynamic or mechanical balance issue:

→
Add rear downforce — rear becomes aerodynamically light at speed without sufficient downforce
→
Add front toe-in — self-centering effect stabilises the front at high speed
→
Stiffen front springs — reduces front dive and bounce at high speed
→
Check ride height — bottoming out creates sudden, dramatic handling changes
→
Stiffen front ARB — reduces lateral instability at the front axle
Car bounces and skips over bumps, losing traction mid-corner
▼

Suspension too stiff for the track surface. Try in this order:

→
Soften bump damping — suspension compresses more slowly, absorbs rather than transmits the bump
→
Soften spring rate at the bouncing end — more travel available to absorb the impact
→
Raise ride height slightly — more suspension travel before hitting bump stops
→
Soften anti-roll bars — ARBs transmit bumpiness across the axle
→
Check tyre pressure — overinflated tyres amplify every road imperfection significantly
Tyres overheating or degrading too quickly mid-race
▼

Thermal degradation — tyre working too hard. Try in this order:

→
Check tyre pressure — overinflated overheats centre; underinflated overheats edges. Read the wear pattern.
→
Adjust camber — incorrect camber makes one part of the tyre do far more work than the rest
→
Use a harder compound — generates less heat at the expense of some peak grip
→
Soften spring rate — less aggressive load transfer = less tyre scrub and heat generation
→
Driving tip: Smooth inputs. Aggressive, jerky inputs generate far more tyre heat than smooth ones
FWD car understeers massively on throttle out of slow corners
▼

FWD torque understeer — front wheels overloaded with drive and steering simultaneously:

→
Soften front ARB — most impactful FWD change; front axle has more traction
→
Stiffen rear ARB — unloads inside rear on exit, helps the car rotate through the corner
→
Increase front negative camber — more cornering contact patch for the driven front tyres
→
Reduce front toe — slight toe-out helps the wheels point into corner during acceleration
→
Driving tip: Use a later apex. Get the car straighter before applying full throttle so the fronts are steering less when they need to drive.
